About Us

According to the WHO, infectious diseases are among the top three leading causes of death worldwide. ASTREMIHA Therapeutics is developing broad-spectrum antivirals aimed at blocking viral replication and strengthening the immune system to combat infections. These next-generation antivirals will address urgent and priority health needs such as AIDS and long COVID, with innovative preventive and curative properties.

Mireille Laforge

PhD, HDR, CR CNRS, CSO, Founder

During many years of research on immunology and infectious diseases, I tried to understand the mechanisms of the physiopathology induced by viruses (HIV/SIV and recently by SARS-CoV-2) as programmed cell death and immune-metabolism dysregulations. I discovered a new family of broad-spectrum antiviral drugs with dual functions in immune modulation that represent a breakthrough in concept and application. These molecules target viral reservoirs (brain and gut) and offer new hope for curing chronic infections and viral reactivation. Several patents have been filed with Inserm-Transfert and licensed to the start-up ASTREMIHA Therapeutics, of which I am the principal founder and scientific director.

My research focuses on understanding the role of immunometabolism in infectious diseases and inflammation to understand how energetic metabolism modulates immune cell functions and impacts the quality of immune responses. I coordinate projects on HIV patients under ART with poor immune responses, pediatric cohorts of children with HIV presenting neurocognitive disorders, and long COVID patients (with 70% presenting neurological symptoms). Our aim is to understand the role of immunometabolism in immune response failure, viral reservoir establishment, and the development of neurocognitive disorders.

Starting in 2025, I will lead the team 3I BRAIN at UMR 1141-NeuroDiderot unit, at Robert Debré Hospital and Child Brain Institute to develop a new immune-metabolic-brain axis to understand the impact of immuno-metabolism modulation on the immune system during brain development, both in physiology and during infections, systemic inflammation, or neuroinflammation. Our research aims to identify common pathways linking dysregulation of the early immature immune system and the development of neurodevelopmental disorders like autism spectrum disorder (ASD) through metabolic pathways. We aim to determine biomarkers of metabolic defects in peripheral blood related to inadequate immune responses and neurodevelopmental disorders. Characterizing these biomarkers could provide new tools for early diagnosis of NDD and propose new treatment strategies through immuno-metabolic modulation and reprogramming.

Pierre Gressens

MD, PhD, DRE INSERM, Co-Founder, Co-CSO

Director of UMR 1141 "NeuroDiderot" Inserm-University of Paris Cité, Pierre Gressens coordinates the Hospital-University Federation "I2-D2" and serves as director of the "PremUP" Foundation. He is also an attached practitioner in the pediatric neurology department (neuropediatrician) at Robert Debré Hospital in Paris.

His translational research focuses on understanding the causes of neurodevelopmental disorders, especially those related to premature birth. He explores the role of glial cells and inflammation, aiming to enhance therapeutic strategies for treating brain injuries resulting from these conditions. Pierre Gressens has authored over 400 original articles, contributing significantly to the field.

Bruno Villoutreix

Drug Development Advisor

Bruno Villoutreix is a research director at INSERM, currently working in the INSERM Unit 1141 at Robert Debré Hospital in Paris.

Previously, he led the INSERM in silico Therapeutic Molecules unit for over 10 years. Bruno has extensive experience at the intersection of molecular medicine, structural bioinformatics, cheminformatics, and artificial intelligence/machine learning. He has worked in various countries including the USA, Finland, Sweden, Bulgaria, and France, in both private and academic sectors.

Bruno Villoutreix has made significant contributions to the development of several pharmacological probes and drug candidates. This includes an in silico designed molecule currently in phase II trials in Sweden for cancer. He has also been involved in developing therapeutic proteins in the field of blood coagulation, designing monoclonal antibodies for cancer treatment, and drug repurposing across multiple therapeutic areas such as infectious diseases and cancer.

Additionally, he is a co-founder of a company in the Boston area, USA, focused on developing targeted therapeutics for vascular barrier disorders.

Michael Spedding

Nicolas Chomont

Scientific Advisor, Immunopathology/Infectiology

Dr. Nicolas Chomont is an Associate Professor in the Department of Microbiology, Infectiology, and Immunology at the University of Montreal and a regular researcher at the CHUM Research Center. He earned his PhD in medical virology from the Pierre and Marie Curie University in Paris, focusing on interactions between HIV and the genital mucosa.

From 2004 to 2009, Dr. Chomont conducted postdoctoral training at the University of Montreal, where he characterized subpopulations of memory CD4 T cells contributing to HIV persistence in individuals under antiretroviral therapy. His subsequent research at the Vaccine & Gene Therapy Institute of Florida focused on further characterizing these reservoirs.

Since 2015, Dr. Chomont has been leading studies at the University of Montreal to elucidate molecular mechanisms involved in HIV latency and to develop new therapeutic strategies for HIV eradication. His research primarily explores immunological mechanisms underlying the persistence of HIV-infected cells, with a specific focus on T lymphocyte proliferation in this context.
